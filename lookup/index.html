What you'll get

When you enter your zip code, we cross-reference it against our database of toss-up congressional races. If your district is one of the 18 competitive seats we're tracking, you'll see:

Your representative

Name, party, district, and whether they're running in 2026.

The money

Lobby endorsement status, funded trips, and campaign contributions from lobbying groups tied to the government of Israel.

The votes

How they voted on key weapons packages sent to the government of Israel — with bill names, dates, and results.

Tailored talking points

Messaging customized to your area's demographics — whether that's faith-based, veteran, suburban, or fiscal accountability framing.

Your challenger

Who's running against the incumbent and what you can do to support accountability.

What you can do

Concrete next steps: download a poster for your district, share with neighbors, volunteer, vote.

What if both candidates support weapons packages?

Write in a name

Most states allow write-in votes for congressional races. You can write in the name of someone who represents your values — a religious figure, a community leader, or simply the words “No More.”

States that prohibit write-ins: Arkansas, Hawaii, Louisiana, Mississippi, Nevada, New Mexico, Oklahoma, South Carolina, South Dakota.

States with easiest write-in rules: Alabama, Iowa, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island (no candidate registration required).

Source: NCSL, individual state election codes. Always verify with your state's Secretary of State.

The point is not whether your write-in candidate wins. The point is that the data will show: a measurable number of voters in this district refused to support either foreign lobby-funded candidate. That data matters. It puts future candidates on notice.
