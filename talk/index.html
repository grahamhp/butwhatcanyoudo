Talking Points

How to talk about this in a way that actually reaches people. Language matters — here's what works.

Why new language matters

Most people agree: their tax dollars shouldn't fund the killing of civilians. But the way this issue has been talked about makes people shut down before they even listen.

Protest slogans feel alienating to ordinary voters. Partisan framing lets people dismiss the message. Jargon loses everyone who isn't already on your side.

This campaign uses plain, factual, nonpartisan language — because we need to reach people who aren't already convinced.

"We don't need to convince activists. We need to convince voters."

The messaging framework

Lead with facts, not feelings

Start with what's provable: the dollar amounts, the vote records, the outcomes. Let people draw their own conclusions. Facts persuade; outrage alienates.

✓ Say: "Your representative accepted $X from the foreign lobby for the government of Israel and voted yes on a $26 billion weapons package. Here's the vote record."

✗ Reconsider: "They're complicit in genocide and they don't care about dead children."

Stay nonpartisan

This is not a left or right issue. Both parties take foreign lobby money. Both parties vote for weapons packages. Always include examples from both sides. Never frame this as a partisan fight.

✓ Say: "Both Republicans and Democrats take money from the foreign lobby for the government of Israel. Thomas Massie, a Republican, was primaried out for voting against it. This isn't about party — it's about accountability."

✗ Reconsider: "Democrats are hypocrites" or "Republicans are warmongers."

Use plain language

Say "the government of Israel" not shorthand. Say "civilians killed" not jargon. Say "your tax dollars" to make it personal. Avoid protest slogans — they shut down the people we need to reach.

✓ Say: "The government of Israel has used American weapons to kill tens of thousands of civilians, including thousands of children."

✗ Reconsider: Protest slogans, chants, or phrases that have been successfully characterized as threatening by opponents of accountability.

Make it local

People care about their rep, their district, their money. Always localize the message: "Your representative took $X from the foreign lobby for the government of Israel and voted yes."

End with action

Every conversation should end with something concrete: look up your rep, share a poster, register to vote, show up in November. Don't leave people feeling hopeless.

Know your audience

Different communities respond to different framings. The core facts are the same — the angle changes. Here's how to adapt your message:

Talking to people of faith

Houses of worship across multiple faiths have been damaged or destroyed with American weapons. These are documented and reported by mainstream media:

  • The Holy Family Catholic Parish in Gaza — the only Catholic church in Gaza — was struck multiple times, killing parishioners and wounding the pastor. (PBS, NBC News)
  • The Church of Saint Porphyrius — a 1,600-year-old Greek Orthodox church, one of the oldest in the world — was hit by an airstrike while hundreds sheltered inside, killing 18 people. (PBS, Fox News)
  • The Gaza Baptist Church — the only Baptist church in the territory — was hit by a tank shell the day after Christmas 2023, while families sheltered inside. (Word&Way, Baptist Press)
  • The Rafi'-Nia Synagogue in Tehran was completely destroyed during an airstrike on Passover 2026, with Torah scrolls left under the rubble. (Newsweek, SBS News)

Catholic, Orthodox, Baptist, and Jewish houses of worship. All hit with American weapons or weapons made possible by American support.

Frame: "As people of faith, we're called to protect the vulnerable, not arm those who attack them. Churches and a synagogue have been bombed. Christian, Muslim, and Jewish communities have all been devastated. Your representative voted to keep sending the weapons that make this possible."

Talking to veterans & military families

Veterans understand rules of engagement. They understand what it means when weapons are used against civilian targets. American-made weapons, paid for with tax dollars, have been used against hospitals and schools.

Frame: "We swore an oath to defend. What's happening violates every standard we were taught. Your representative voted to send weapons with no conditions on how they're used."

Talking to suburban parents

Over 10,000 children have been killed in Gaza since October 2023, according to the UN. Hospitals and schools have been destroyed. These are facts, not opinions.

Frame: "Thousands of children have been killed with weapons your representative voted to send. When your children ask what you did — what will you say? You can't control what happens overseas. But you can control your vote."

Talking to fiscal conservatives

Congress approved $26.38 billion in aid to the government of Israel in April 2024 alone (H.R. 8034, passed 366-58). That's on top of $3.8 billion sent every year as baseline aid.

Frame: "$26 billion of your tax dollars to a foreign government — while American bridges crumble, schools are underfunded, and veterans wait months for care. Your representative chose a foreign lobby over your community."

Talking to college students & young voters

Every generation faces a moral test. Previous generations asked "how did people let it happen?" — now this generation knows. The question is what they do about it.

Frame: "This isn't ancient history — it's happening now, with weapons your taxes paid for. You can vote in November. It takes 10 minutes. And it matters more than you think."

Talking to libertarians & small-government voters

The government takes your money and sends it to arm a foreign country — no strings attached, no accountability, no oversight. The foreign lobby for the government of Israel spent $126.9 million in the 2024 cycle alone to make sure it stays that way.

Frame: "No oversight on how the weapons are used. No accountability when civilians die. Your representative didn't ask you — they just voted yes and took the foreign lobby money. This is a government accountability issue."

Why some approaches backfire

You can say whatever you want. But if the goal is reaching the people we need to reach — people who aren't already on our side — some approaches work better than others. Here's what we've found shuts people down before they can hear the message:

  • Less effective: Protest chants and slogans — They signal "activist" and the people we need to reach tend to tune out before listening.
  • Less effective: Partisan framing — The moment it sounds like one party blaming another, half the room stops listening. Both parties are complicit. Say so.
  • Less effective: Academic jargon — If a term needs explanation, it's a barrier. Plain language reaches more people.
  • Less effective: Personal attacks — Attacking the person makes it easy to dismiss you. Attacking the votes and the money — that's harder to argue with.
  • Less effective: Conflating a government with a people — Always say "the government of Israel," never just "Israel." This makes the distinction between a government's actions and Jewish people crystal clear. It also makes the argument harder to dismiss as antisemitic.

Sample conversations

Real-world examples of how to navigate common objections and redirect to the facts.

When someone says: "But it's complicated"

"I don't know enough about the history to have an opinion. It's really complicated."

"The history is complicated. But the question isn't about history — it's about right now. Your representative voted to send $26 billion in weapons to a government that has killed tens of thousands of civilians. That's a fact, not an opinion. Do you want your tax dollars doing that? If not, you can vote in November. That's what this is about."

When someone says: "Isn't this antisemitic?"

"This sounds like it could be anti-Jewish to me."

"This is about a government's actions, not a religion or a people. We're talking about the government of Israel — just like we'd talk about any government whose military actions are funded by our tax dollars. Many Jewish Americans oppose these weapons transfers. Organizations like Jewish Voice for Peace and IfNotNow have been leading this conversation. Criticizing a government's military policy isn't antisemitism — it's democracy."

When someone says: "But what about Hamas?"

"What about the attacks? Don't they have a right to defend themselves?"

"Nobody is defending attacks on civilians — on any side. The question is: should the US Congress keep sending billions in weapons that have been used to kill tens of thousands of civilians, including thousands of children? Self-defense doesn't require destroying hospitals and schools. Our tax dollars, our choice. That's what the vote in November is about."

When someone says: "My vote doesn't matter"

"Nothing I do makes a difference. The system is rigged."

"Mariannette Miller-Meeks won her last House race by six votes. Six. The foreign lobby for the government of Israel spent $32 million trying to defeat Thomas Massie — because they know every vote matters. And Zohran Mamdani beat Andrew Cuomo for NYC mayor despite being massively outspent. Your vote matters. The foreign lobby knows it. That's why they spend millions to make you think it doesn't."

When someone says: "I'm not political"

"I don't really follow politics."

"You don't have to be political. You just have to care whether your tax money is being used to bomb hospitals and kill children. If that bothers you — and it bothers most people — then voting takes 10 minutes. Go to butwhatcanyoudo.org, type in your zip code, and it'll tell you exactly who your rep is and what they voted for. That's it. Ten minutes."

It's already happening

Both parties tried to silence dissent on this issue. In both cases, voters pushed back.

Thomas Massie, a Republican congressman from Kentucky, started voting against weapons packages to the government of Israel as the civilian death toll climbed. The Republican establishment spent heavily to primary him out — removing one of their own for breaking ranks.

In New York, the Democratic establishment did the same thing — pouring millions into a candidate to stop Zohran Mamdani, a Democrat who opposed the weapons packages. Voters chose Mamdani anyway.

The establishment will fight back. But this issue isn't going away. And when enough people show up, they win.
